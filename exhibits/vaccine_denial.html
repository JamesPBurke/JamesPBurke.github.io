Trump Administration Actions Undermining Measles Vaccination

A Comprehensive Analysis of Statements and Policies Contributing to Vaccine Skepticism

How Federal Officials Have Undermined Confidence in the MMR Vaccine

This presentation documents actions taken by Trump administration officials—both before and during their time in office—that have contributed to measles vaccine skepticism and undermined public health recommendations (Hancock, 2026; Stat News, 2019).

Context: South Carolina measles outbreak (Oct 2025–Feb 2026) reached 900+ cases,
the largest U.S. measles outbreak since elimination in 2000 (CIDRAP, 2026; South Carolina DPH, 2026).

Donald Trump's Vaccine Skepticism (2012–2016)

Twitter Statements Linking Vaccines to Autism

August 2012

"Massive combined inoculations to small children is the cause for big increase in autism... Spread shots over long period and watch positive result" (ABC News, 2012).

March 2014

"Healthy young child goes to doctor, gets pumped with massive shot of many vaccines, doesn't feel good and changes – AUTISM. Many such cases!" (ABC News, 2019).

September 2014

"Autism WAY UP – I believe in vaccinations but not massive, all at once, shots. Too much for small child to handle. Govt. should stop NOW!" (ABC News, 2012).

Meeting with Anti-Vaccine Activists

August 2016

Trump held a 45‑minute meeting with discredited MMR‑autism researcher Andrew Wakefield and other anti‑vaccine activists at a Florida fundraiser, promising to watch Vaxxed and expressing interest in further meetings (Wadman, 2016; History of Vaccines, 2016).

These actions helped establish Trump's credibility with the anti‑vaccine movement before he took office (History of Vaccines, 2016).

First Trump Administration (2017–2020)

January 2017: RFK Jr. Vaccine Commission Talk

January 10, 2017

Trump met with Robert F. Kennedy Jr. to discuss a commission on "vaccine safety and scientific integrity"; Kennedy said Trump "has some doubts about the current vaccine policies, and he has questions about it" (CIDRAP, 2017; NPR, 2017).

This signaled potential federal backing for vaccine skepticism at the start of his presidency (NPR, 2017).

April 2019: Mixed Messaging During Measles Outbreak

April 26, 2019

During a major U.S. measles surge, Trump stated: "They have to get the shots. The vaccinations are so important," reversing years of public claims linking vaccines to autism (ABC News, 2019; Stat News, 2019).

In 2019, the U.S. nearly lost measles elimination status because of large outbreaks in under‑vaccinated communities (OPB, 2026; CDC, 2026).

Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s Anti‑Vaccine Record

Children’s Health Defense and Anti‑Vaccine Campaigning

  • Founded and led Children’s Health Defense, a prominent anti‑vaccine organization (The Lancet, 2024; CAP, 2025).
  • As a 2024 presidential candidate, stated "There’s no vaccine that is safe and effective" and promoted claims that vaccines cause autism (The Lancet, 2024).
  • Has urged parents not to vaccinate and framed himself as "saving" children from vaccines (The Lancet, 2024).

The Samoa Measles Tragedy

Kennedy visited Samoa in 2019 to meet with the prime minister and anti‑vaccine activists (U.S. News, 2025).

Shortly after, Samoa suffered a measles outbreak that killed 83 people, mostly infants and children, amid falling vaccination coverage (U.S. News, 2025; Wikipedia, 2019).

Samoan officials later said Kennedy’s visit bolstered local anti‑vaccine activists’ credibility (U.S. News, 2025).

COVID‑19 Vaccine Misinformation

  • Called COVID‑19 vaccines a "crime against humanity" and "the deadliest vaccine ever made" (The Lancet, 2024).
  • Promoted books and media falsely claiming COVID‑19 vaccines caused a wave of sudden deaths among young people (The Lancet, 2024).

2025: Undermining Measles Vaccine During Texas Outbreak

Texas measles outbreak, Jan–Aug 2025: 762 cases and 2 child deaths, largely among unvaccinated people (Pulmonology Advisor, 2025; Stat News, 2025).

Kennedy's Misleading Claims

April 3, 2025 – Fox News

Kennedy said, "When you and I were kids, everybody got measles… The vaccine doesn’t do that… For many people, it wanes," implying natural infection is superior to vaccination (Stat News, 2025).

Expert Response

Dr. Paul Offit explained that if vaccine‑induced immunity waned substantially, measles would not have been eliminated in 2000 and that immune memory provides durable protection (Stat News, 2025).

Dr. Michael Mina noted that the outbreak was primarily driven by unvaccinated individuals and that even waning immunity is unlikely to generate large viral spread (Stat News, 2025).

Kennedy emphasized "natural immunity" and questioned MMR effectiveness while a measles outbreak was killing children in Texas (Pulmonology Advisor, 2025).

May 2025: Removing COVID Vaccines from Childhood Schedule

The Decision

May 2025

HHS, led by Kennedy with NIH Director Jay Bhattacharya and FDA Commissioner Marty Makary, removed COVID‑19 vaccines from the childhood schedule and from recommendations for pregnant women, restricting them mainly to adults 65+ and high‑risk groups (First Focus, 2025).

Misleading Rationale

  • Officials claimed "most countries" do not recommend COVID‑19 vaccines for children, but cited examples showed these countries still recommend primary vaccination for children while limiting yearly boosters (First Focus, 2025).
  • Policy changes made COVID‑19 vaccines more expensive and harder to obtain, as insurers may no longer cover them and prescriptions may be required (First Focus, 2025).
Restricting one vaccine on weak grounds undermines confidence in the entire childhood schedule, including MMR (Buttenheim, 2025).

June 2025: Destroying the Vaccine Advisory System

Purging ACIP

June 9, 2025

Kennedy fired all 17 members of the CDC’s Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and replaced them with a smaller group that included vaccine skeptics and individuals without relevant expertise (Global Biodefense, 2025a; Families USA, 2025).

Experts called the ACIP purge "absolutely unprecedented" and warned it posed a "level‑4 alarm" for vaccine policy (Global Biodefense, 2025a).

Who Was Appointed?

New appointees included self‑described "anti‑vaxxer" Kimberly Biss and pediatric cardiologist Kirk Milhoan, as well as individuals tied to anti‑vaccine litigation and COVID‑19 misinformation (Biopharm International, 2025).

Immediate Agenda

June 25–26, 2025

The reconstituted ACIP revived debates over thimerosal‑containing flu vaccines, echoing long‑debunked autism claims (Global Biodefense, 2025a).

September 2025: Trump Links Vaccines and Tylenol to Autism

The White House Briefing

September 22, 2025

At a White House briefing, Trump told pregnant women not to take Tylenol and suggested vaccines and Tylenol contribute to autism, while urging parents not to let doctors "pump" babies full of shots (Lin et al., 2025; ABC News, 2025a).

  • He advised delaying the hepatitis B birth dose until age 12 and promoted leucovorin as an "autism treatment" despite limited early research (ABC News, 2025a).
These remarks publicly endorsed a debunked vaccine‑autism narrative during ongoing measles outbreaks (Autism Science Foundation, 2025).

Scientific Pushback

Autism researchers and advocacy organizations stressed that decades of studies show no causal link between vaccines and autism, and warned that discouraging Tylenol use could harm pregnant people and children (ABC News, 2025a; ABC7 News, 2025).

November 2025: CDC Removes "Vaccines Do Not Cause Autism"

Kennedy’s Direct Order

November 2025

Kennedy directed CDC to remove language asserting that vaccines do not cause autism and to replace it with text framing that conclusion as not "evidence‑based," despite extensive research (Bogel-Burroughs & Stolberg, 2025).

Breaking a Confirmation Promise

Senator Bill Cassidy stated that Kennedy previously promised not to change this messaging; Kennedy later acknowledged that Cassidy "disagreed" but implemented the change anyway (Bogel-Burroughs & Stolberg, 2025).

Rolling back CDC’s clear statement that vaccines do not cause autism gave official cover to anti‑vaccine narratives and undermined public health messaging (TODAY, 2025).

CDC Principal Deputy Director

Dr. Jay Bhattacharya

Dr. Marty Makary
FDA Commissioner

Called CDC’s vaccine advisory panel a "kangaroo court" and accused the previous administration of withholding myocarditis data while defending cuts to vaccine recommendations (Facher, 2025; PBS NewsHour, 2025).

January 2026: Overhauling the Childhood Vaccine Schedule

The Changes

January 4–5, 2026

Acting CDC Director Jim O’Neill, following a presidential memorandum, cut universally recommended vaccines from 17 to 11 diseases and moved six others into "high‑risk" or "shared decision‑making" categories (CDC, 2026; HHS, 2026).

Measles vaccine remained in the "core 11," but the overhaul signaled that long‑standing recommendations are more politically negotiable (CDC, 2026).

Process Concerns

Public health legal experts warned that bypassing normal ACIP deliberations and Administrative Procedure Act requirements risks arbitrary decision‑making and erodes trust (Families USA, 2025).

Insurance Implications

  • Because many insurers only must fully cover vaccines that ACIP recommends for all children, downgrading vaccines could result in new out‑of‑pocket costs and lower uptake (Families USA, 2025; Biopharm International, 2025).

December 2025: Rolling Back Hepatitis B Birth Dose

ACIP Vote

December 2025

The new ACIP voted 8–3 to limit hepatitis B vaccination at birth to infants born to mothers who are hepatitis B–positive or whose status is unknown, reversing over 30 years of universal birth‑dose practice (Lupkin, 2025b).

Expert Objections

Pediatric infectious‑disease experts questioned changing a policy that had dramatically reduced childhood hepatitis B based on theoretical concerns instead of clear safety data (Lupkin, 2025b).

Weakening a long‑standing newborn vaccine recommendation signals that core vaccines can be rolled back, feeding broader skepticism (Families USA, 2025).

By early February 2026, South Carolina reported more than 900 measles cases, mostly among unvaccinated or under‑vaccinated individuals, with multiple hospitalizations (CIDRAP, 2026; South Carolina DPH, 2026).

Delayed and Defensive Federal Response

HHS issued a statement acknowledging the crisis but blamed declining vaccination rates and eroding trust on prior administrations and the COVID era, despite the current team’s own record of vaccine skepticism (Protect Our Care, 2026).

Abraham’s Comments

CDC Principal Deputy Director Ralph Abraham downplayed the significance of losing measles elimination status and framed outbreaks as consequences of "choice" and border realities (Protect Our Care, 2026).

Mixed Signals from Oz

CMS Administrator Dr. Mehmet Oz eventually urged people to "take the vaccine" for measles, while simultaneously defending Kennedy and Trump as advocates for vaccine safety (PBS NewsHour, 2026).

Impact on Public Trust and Vaccine Uptake

Parent Attitudes

A September 2025 KFF–Washington Post survey found that only 14% of parents had "a lot" of confidence in CDC and FDA to ensure vaccine safety, and 16% had delayed or skipped at least one childhood vaccine (Hamel et al., 2025).

Vaccine skipping was more common among Republican parents, MAGA supporters, and homeschooling families (Hamel et al., 2025).

Research on Trump’s Influence

A study found that Trump supporters exposed to his anti‑vaccination tweets showed increased vaccine concern, while non‑supporters did not, demonstrating a leader‑driven effect on hesitancy (Hornsey et al., 2020).

Clinicians’ Experience

Pediatricians report rising vaccine hesitancy since Kennedy assumed HHS leadership, with more families questioning routine shots and expressing confusion about conflicting messages (TODAY, 2025; Autism Science Foundation, 2025).
