First Trump Administration (2017–2020)

January 2017: RFK Jr. Vaccine Commission Talk

January 10, 2017

Trump met with Robert F. Kennedy Jr. to discuss a commission on "vaccine safety and scientific integrity"; Kennedy said Trump "has some doubts about the current vaccine policies, and he has questions about it."[8]

This signaled potential federal backing for vaccine skepticism at the start of his presidency.[9]

April 2019: Mixed Messaging During Measles Outbreak

April 26, 2019

During a major U.S. measles surge, Trump told reporters: "They have to get the shots. The vaccinations are so important," reversing years of public claims linking vaccines to autism.[10]

2019: U.S. nearly lost measles elimination status because of large outbreaks in under‑vaccinated communities.[11]

Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s Anti‑Vaccine Record

Children’s Health Defense and Anti‑Vaccine Campaigning

  • Founded and led Children’s Health Defense, a leading anti‑vaccine organization.[12]
  • As a 2024 presidential candidate, stated: "There’s no vaccine that is safe and effective," and promoted claims that vaccines cause autism.[13]
  • Has repeatedly urged parents to avoid vaccinating their children and framed himself as "saving" them from vaccines.[13]

The Samoa Measles Tragedy

Kennedy visited Samoa in 2019 at the invitation of a local anti‑vaccine influencer, meeting with the prime minister and anti‑vaccine activists.[14]

Subsequently, Samoa suffered a measles outbreak that killed 83 people, mostly infants and children, amid plummeting vaccination coverage.[15]

Samoan health officials later said Kennedy’s visit helped bolster local anti‑vaccine activists’ credibility before the outbreak.[14]

COVID‑19 Vaccine Misinformation

  • Called COVID‑19 vaccines a "crime against humanity" and "the deadliest vaccine ever made," in contrast to data showing millions of lives saved.[13]
  • Promoted books and media falsely claiming COVID vaccines caused a wave of sudden deaths in young people.[13]

2025: Undermining Measles Vaccine During Texas Outbreak

Texas measles outbreak, Jan–Aug 2025:
762 cases and 2 child deaths, largely among unvaccinated people.[16]

Kennedy's Misleading Claims

April 3, 2025 – Fox News

Kennedy said: "When you and I were kids, everybody got measles, and measles gave you lifetime protection… The [MMR] vaccine doesn’t do that… For many people, it wanes," suggesting natural infection was superior to vaccination.[17]

Expert Response

Dr. Paul Offit countered that if vaccine‑induced immunity waned substantially, measles would not have been eliminated in 2000 and that immune memory cells provide durable protection.[17]

Dr. Michael Mina noted the Texas outbreak was primarily driven by unvaccinated individuals and that even people with modestly waning immunity are unlikely to transmit large amounts of virus.[17]

Kennedy was emphasizing "natural immunity" and sowing doubt about MMR effectiveness while a measles outbreak was killing children in Texas.[17]

May 2025: Removing COVID Vaccines from Childhood Schedule

The Decision

May 2025

HHS, led by Kennedy with NIH Director Jay Bhattacharya and FDA Commissioner Marty Makary, removed COVID‑19 vaccines from the childhood immunization schedule and from recommendations for pregnant women, restricting them mainly to adults 65+ and high‑risk groups.[18]

Misleading Rationale

  • Officials claimed "most countries" don’t recommend COVID vaccines for children and that peer nations only vaccinate older adults; analyses showed these countries still recommend primary COVID vaccination for children but not annual boosters.[18]
  • Policy made COVID vaccines more expensive and harder to obtain for families, as insurers may no longer cover them and prescriptions may be required.[18]
Restricting one vaccine on weak grounds erodes confidence in the entire childhood schedule, including MMR.[19]

June 2025: Destroying the Vaccine Advisory System

Purging ACIP

June 9, 2025

Kennedy fired all 17 expert members of the CDC’s Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP), replacing them with eight appointees, many aligned with anti‑vaccine ideologies and lacking relevant expertise.[20]

ACIP had provided nonpartisan, evidence‑based vaccine recommendations under both parties since 1964; experts called the purge "absolutely unprecedented" and a "red‑alert, level 4 alarm."[20]

Who Was Appointed?

New members included self‑described "anti‑vaxxer" obstetrician Kimberly Biss and pediatric cardiologist Kirk Milhoan, along with figures tied to anti‑vaccine organizations and COVID‑19 misinformation.[21]

Immediate Agenda

June 25–26, 2025

The reconstituted ACIP put thimerosal‑containing flu vaccines on the agenda, resurrecting long‑debunked conspiracy theories linking the preservative to autism.[21]

September 2025: Trump Links Vaccines and Tylenol to Autism

The White House Briefing

September 22, 2025

Standing beside HHS Secretary Kennedy, Trump told pregnant women "don’t take Tylenol" and said "they pump so much stuff into those beautiful little babies, it’s a disgrace," alleging links between Tylenol, vaccines, and autism without evidence.[22]

  • Trump suggested delaying the hepatitis B birth dose until age 12 and promoted leucovorin as an "autism treatment" despite limited early research.[23]
This was a presidential endorsement of a debunked vaccine‑autism narrative during ongoing measles outbreaks.[22]

Scientific Pushback

Autism researchers and organizations stressed that decades of studies show no causal link between vaccines and autism, and warned that discouraging Tylenol use could be harmful by leaving fevers untreated.[23]

November 2025: CDC Removes “Vaccines Do Not Cause Autism”

Kennedy’s Direct Order

November 2025

Kennedy instructed CDC to remove longstanding language stating that vaccines do not cause autism and to replace it with wording suggesting that conclusion is not "evidence‑based," despite decades of research.[24]

Breaking a Confirmation Promise

Sen. Bill Cassidy said Kennedy had promised during confirmation hearings not to remove that language; Kennedy acknowledged Cassidy "disagreed" with the change but proceeded anyway.[24]

Reversing CDC’s clear statement that vaccines don’t cause autism gave official cover to anti‑vaccine narratives and undermined public health messaging.[24]

January 2026: Overhauling the Childhood Vaccine Schedule

The Changes

January 4–5, 2026

Acting CDC Director Jim O’Neill signed a decision memorandum, following a Trump presidential directive, cutting universally recommended childhood vaccines from 17 diseases to 11 and moving six vaccines into "high‑risk" or "shared decision‑making" categories.[29]

Measles vaccine stayed in the "core 11," but the overall message signaled that long‑standing recommendations are negotiable.[30]

Process Concerns

Public health legal experts warned the changes bypassed normal ACIP deliberation and Administrative Procedure Act norms, risking "arbitrary and capricious" decision‑making and undermining trust.[29]

Insurance Implications

  • Under federal law, many insurers only must fully cover vaccines that ACIP recommends for all children; downgrading vaccines can lead to out‑of‑pocket costs and lower uptake.[31]

December 2025: Rolling Back Hepatitis B Birth Dose

ACIP Vote

December 2025

The reconstituted ACIP voted 8–3 to narrow hepatitis B recommendations at birth, limiting universal newborn immunization to infants born to mothers who are hepatitis B–positive or whose status is unknown, reversing a 30‑year policy.[32]

Expert Objections

Representatives from pediatric infectious‑disease societies questioned why the committee was changing a policy that dramatically reduced hepatitis B in children based on theoretical concerns rather than clear safety data.[32]

Weakening a long‑standing birth‑dose vaccine recommendation signals that core childhood vaccines can be easily rolled back, feeding broader skepticism.[31]

January 2026: ACIP Chair Questions Measles and Polio Vaccines

Kirk Milhoan’s Remarks

January 23, 2026

On a podcast, ACIP chair Kirk Milhoan questioned whether Americans still need the polio vaccine and said key vaccines like polio and measles should be optional, prioritizing individual autonomy over public health goals.[33]

Milhoan argued that vaccine safety has been inadequately studied and dismissed vaccine‑safety monitoring systems as "very poor," despite extensive surveillance infrastructure.[33]

The federal vaccine advisory chair casting doubt on basic vaccines and safety systems directly undermines public confidence in MMR and other childhood shots.[34]

2025–26: Failed Response to South Carolina Measles Outbreak

By early February 2026, South Carolina reported 900+ measles cases, with most patients unvaccinated or under‑vaccinated and multiple hospitalizations.[35]

Delayed and Defensive Federal Response

January 27, 2026

HHS issued a statement acknowledging the crisis but blamed declining vaccination rates and erosion of trust on prior administrations and the COVID era, despite the current team’s own history of anti‑vaccine messaging.[36]

Abraham’s Comments

CDC Principal Deputy Director Ralph Abraham said losing measles elimination status would "not really" matter and framed outbreaks as an expected consequence of communities choosing not to vaccinate.[35]

Mixed Signals from Oz

CMS head Dr. Mehmet Oz eventually urged: "Take the [measles] vaccine, please," yet publicly defended Kennedy and Trump as champions of vaccine safety, blurring the administration’s message.[37]

Impact on Public Trust and Vaccine Uptake

Parent Attitudes

A September 2025 KFF–Washington Post survey found fewer than 1 in 6 parents (14%) had "a lot" of confidence in CDC and FDA to ensure vaccine safety and effectiveness.[38]

One in six parents (16%) reported skipping or delaying at least one recommended childhood vaccine (excluding flu/COVID); rates were higher among Republican and MAGA‑identifying parents and among homeschoolers.[38]

Research on Trump’s Influence

A Journal of Experimental Social Psychology study found that Trump supporters exposed to his anti‑vaccine tweets showed significantly increased vaccine concern, while non‑supporters did not, demonstrating a leader‑driven effect on hesitancy.[39]

Clinicians’ Experience

Pediatricians report rising vaccine hesitancy since Kennedy took over HHS, with more families questioning routine shots and expressing confusion about conflicting messages from federal leaders and clinicians.[40]
