In my morning briefing, I encounteres the following article and had a small, intial emotional reaction, knowing what it would be about:

[Why a Doorway Greeting May Be One of the Most Underrated Classroom Strategies - Laura Mooiman, the74](https://www.the74million.org/article/why-a-doorway-greeting-may-be-one-of-the-most-underrated-classroom-strategies/)

As the article suggests, educators might be hesitant about the idea of meeting with students for an additional performance. I recall initially thinking this would be ideal for younger grades, where teachers interact with a smaller number of students daily and might have fewer "handshakes" to remember.

This isn't something I do. It's not _really_ my personality. I first saw this video a couple of years before I became a classroom teacher. My thinking at that time was that high school students are transitioning to adulthood, and the expectation is that they should also be transitioning to more professional behavior.

I'm a firm believer that playfulness has a powerful place in our learning spaces and even in the world of work. It's clear to me that the type of playfulness I engage in is constrained by my personality and my personal inhibitions, and this influenced my justifications around this strategy. If this was truly good for younger kids, why not for older kids as well? 

Mooiman's piece addresses an aspect of this with her distilling down of the handshake to a minimalist greeting that hits the most important points: 

>Teachers used the student’s name. They made eye contact. They offered a brief nonverbal greeting — a handshake, fist bump, high five, nod or wave. Then they added a short positive or “pre-corrective” statement, which is simply a friendly reminder of what to do next.

That's it.

I said earlier that I don't do this. Every teacher has a style, and this hasn't really been mine. But I have stood in the hall while colleagues did some version of this, and I marveled at their memory for names and their social engagement. For those of us who struggle with social engagement in general, it's a big deal. I'm in my classroom trying to collect myself, usually switching from one curriculum to another or cleaning up after the previous students. I'm not really prepared to be super social in those instances, though I engage cordially with students entering the classroom. Additionally, I'm masking indoors, which makes me self-conscious. A topic for another time. 

I decided to write this piece to add this brief perspective: my informal observation tells me that this engagement can be good for both the students and the teacher. Coming across this article made me reflect, and review what I've seen in school. There are more student behavior issues than I ever expected. There are higher student needs, I'm told, than ever before. And it certainly feels that way. Addressing students' social emotional learning needs isn't really an either/or choice
