## Can you vote to revive Elvis from the dead?

That's a silly example, but many people across the Commonwealth of Massachusetts are acting as though they believe there are no constraints on anything that voters approve on a ballot.

*Yes.* You *have the right* to and *could* vote to exhume Elvis' remains, transport them to Dr. Frankenstein's lab, where they would be grafted onto parts from more recently deceased performers. As long as proponents could get it on the ballot, you could vote for it.

But what happens next? I think it's clear to most people what happens next, but social media is full of people acting as though they think the legislature would have to start buying shovels.

## Question 1, 2024

In 2024, Massachusetts voters approved a [question](https://www.sec.state.ma.us/divisions/elections/publications/information-for-voters-24/quest_1.htm) against the advice of constitutional scholars and civics educators:

> This proposed law would specify that the State Auditor has the authority to audit the Legislature.

The "Against" entry on the ballot wrote:

> A legislative audit conducted by the State Auditor, who is an executive branch official, without the Legislature’s consent would violate the separation of powers and legislative supremacy described in and required by the Massachusetts Constitution.

I don't recall that there was much of an effort to educate people on this ballot question, but it's clear from people's reaction after the fact that people have been happy to rely on a relatively straightforward understanding of the consequences of this vote, uncomplicated by a grasp of the state constitution. People who voted for this were led to believe, or possibly just happened to believe, one or more of the following things:

- there currently is no audit of the legislature
- if there is an audit of the legislature, it's not available to citizens
- if there is an audit of the legislature, and it's hidden, it might be corrupt because nobody can check it
- the state auditor has the authority to audit anything in the state government, but the legislature has been unlawfully refusing to be audited ([the ballot question's "FOR" argument implied this](https://www.sec.state.ma.us/divisions/elections/publications/information-for-voters-24/quest_1.htm))

None of these things were the case[^1]. But you can see how people might think they were if they are just regular citizens. So it's easy to explain why over 70% of voters approved the question. And, perhaps, easy to see why some things aren't great things to have the general public voting on[^2].

## Too Bad. We Voted For It

Let me hit you with a hypothetical. And I'm assuming here that you thought my Elvis example was "not the same thing" or intentionally extreme.

What if, just before an election, it came to light that a specific person did something extremely unpopular. Let's say that they did something so unpopular that a TV station poll found 95% of people in the state wer ein favor of running this person out of state. Let's say, for the sake of imagining this situation, that the person in question is *you*.

Now, you can't force someone to leave the state, right? But someone comes up with a ballot question at the right time asking people to approve of a law that would take away your house and give it to the other people in your neighborhood because they're the ones who have had to put up with you. You are shocked and upset, and you believe it will never pass a vote.

But then, it does!

I guess you're out of a home. I am sorry to see you go.
