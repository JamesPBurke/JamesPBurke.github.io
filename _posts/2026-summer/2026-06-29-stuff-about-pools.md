My pool is finally about to be opened for the year. Even though this is something I do every year, it sometimes takes me a while to remember what I do each year to make pool-opening happen. I'm going to use this post to record some information I know I'm going to want to reference sometime in the future. And maybe you'll find it useful as well.

If you already know a lot about pools, maybe go straight to the paragraphs in **green**{: style="color: green"}. That's where I've indicated some things I've learned most recently, which might be basics for others, but it took me a while to get to.

# About My Pool

Specs of the pool are at the end of this entry, but I have an approximately 7200-gallon above-ground Doughboy pool. Sand Filter. Chlorine sanitized. If you need to know how many gallons your pool is, you can look up the formulas, or use an online calculator like the one built in to [Pool Math](https://www.troublefreepool.com/calc.html). 

# Filtration

We have a sand setup for filtration. The internals can break, but sand filtration is generally easy and efficient. Sand only needs to be replaced maybe every 5-10 years. There are options to improve filtration including adding diatomaceous earth that acts to fill some of the gaps in the sand, increasing the filtration efficiency. There are also sand substitutes/enhancers like cotton-like balls and cellulose fibers, meant to capture junk better. Some chemicals also affect filtration (discussed in the [chemical philosophy](#chemical-philosophy) section)

## Pool Pump Speed

When I replaced my original 1 HP Hayward pump, I opted for a variable speed pump capable of 1.5 HP. And for about 2 years, I've been using the pump on and off at its maximum speed. At max speed it pushes 80 gallons/min or so. This is too much for my filter, and I've learned that it is unnecessary. At slow speed it will push 40 gallons/min. The difference in pool water turnover is that high speed will filter my entire pool in 1.5 hours, low speed wakes 3 hours. But low speed also uses approximately 1/8 the electricity.

You can save money with a variable speed pump by running it on low. Better still, it filters fine particles more effectively on low, as it is less likely to break up clumps and push them through the filter. I didn't know any of this before today, so now I plan to run my pump almost entirely on low, except for when I'm vacuuming or back-washing.

## Filtering With Minimal Electricity

Figure out your pool's volume (PV) using math, or an [online pool volume calculator](https://www.google.com/search?q=pool+volume+calculator). Look up how many gallons per minute (GPM) your pump can pump. 

Figure out how long (in hours) it takes your pump to force your entire pool through the filter (TT) using this formula.

$$\boxed{TT = \frac{PV}{GPM * 60}}$$
{: style="color:green"}

It's recommended that you pump your pool water completely through the filter once per day. In my case, on high speed, that will take 90 minutes. On low it will take 3 hours. That's your baseline. On a normal day, don't run your pump longer than that. If you've had a pool party, heavy rainstorm, or some other event that may have gotten organic matter into the pool, increase your time by 50% or even more if something dramatic happened. 
{: style="color:green"}

It's likely that this formula is giving you a lower number than you were recommended to run your pump. That's because people are erring way out on the side of caution and without knowing your pool size or pump rate. Save yourself some electricity, do the math, and pump a little less.

# Chemical Philosophy

I have mostly adopted [Trouble Free Pool](https://www.troublefreepool.com/blog/2018/12/12/abcs-of-pool-water-chemistry/)'s philosophy on pool chemistry. That means I use a simple set of generic chemicals, most of which don't have brand names. They're entirely sufficient anf this also keeps costs down. ALso, I don't generally use "algicides." Chlorine is a sanitizer, and powerful oxidizer, and that's what kills the algae in my pool. 

- Liquid chlorine for sanitizing (12% strength from local sources, but basic supermarket bleach is also effective, though less concentrated, so you'll need more)
- Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) to raise pH and Total Alkalinity
- Muratic Acid to lower pH (especially needed if a lot of organic stuff has fallen into he pool)
- Water (it's a chemical)

I recommend you visit [Trouble Free Pool](https://www.troublefreepool.com) and check out their Pool School. You will learn a lot in a short amount of time, and it could save you a lot of cash. I've only paid attention to the recommendations there for chlorinated pools, but they give info on SWG (salt water generator) systems, which essentially maintain a level of chlorine using sodium chloride (table salt). 

They helpfully offer a [Pool Chemistry Calculator](https://www.troublefreepool.com/calc.html) that will tell you exactly how much chemistry to use in your pool. TO get accurate measurements of your pool chemistry, you're going to need a [TF test kit](https://tftestkits.com/). The TF-100 had everything I needed for my above-ground pool. You'll use the kit for years, and you can get refills when you run out of a reagent. Good testing saves you from wasting money on unnecessary chemicals.

# Closing

It's important to close the pool properly because freezing water can damage almost every part of the system. Here's a checklist of the most important things to take care of:

- Water completely drained out of the sand filter
- Multiport set to Winter
- Return covered with plastic disc insert under the twist-off collar
- Pump brought indoors
  - Pump hardware (screws) placed in a baggie
- Aquadoor installed to keep water in the pool and out of the skimmer (ice can crack skimmer)
- Skimmer basket brought inside
- Pool cover installed

If pool was properly cleaned and chlorinated late enough in the season, algae doesn't form over the winter, but it will start to green in April. So even if pool isn't covered over winter, covering it in late March may keep the water clear. Lazy backup strategy.

# Opening

If pool was closed properly, opening is simply a matter of getting the pool cover off (requiring pumping out the water that accumulated) and then attending to the chemicals:

- add chlorine up to maintenance level
- adjust stabilizer (to about 30 ppm)
- Check and fix pH
- Check and fix total alkalinity
- if pool fails OCLT
  - SLAM (shock level and maintain) until it passes an overnight chlorine loss test (OCLT - pass if there's no or minimal chlorine loss overnight) and the captured chlorine is at or below 0.5 ppm.
- Vacuum or rake or otherwise get rid of detritus
- When pool is clear, swim

In cases where the pool is murky, green, or green-black, SLAM immediately after you get the stabilizer to 30, and pH is in the target range. Once you get rid of the green, the rest of the job is just filtering, as long as the equipment is working properly.

SOURCE: [Trouble-Free Pools](https://www.troublefreepool.com/blog/2019/01/18/opening-your-winterized-pool/)


# Miscellaneous

General above-ground-pool helpful knowledge

## Muratic acid 20° Bé (Baumé rating) to bring pH down

It does it very quickly. Great if you're trying to get the pH down to start killing algae.

## Pool First Aid 

The product [Pool First Aid](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0D1RQ5381?th=1) is a clarifier and some enzymes which are supposed to help break down organic substances and oils. Trouble Free Pools will tell you it's unnecessary, and it probably is. But it can save you time in cases where you're unable to get the filter to filter very fine particles fast enough. Other clarifiers probably also do this. Maybe cheaper as well.

## Quick-Reference Multiport Valve Cheat Sheet
* **FILTER:** Default operational setting. Run on **Low Speed (40 GPM)** for standard daily maintenance and fine particle polishing.
* **BACKWASH:** Run on **High Speed** until the sight glass begins to transition from cloudy/milky to translucent (typically 30–45 seconds during heavy clearing).
* **RINSE:** **Mandatory 15-second run** after every single backwash. Resets the loose sand bed to prevent a dirty plume from shooting back into the pool. *Note: This setting features maximum internal valve friction (head loss).*
