# Teaching Code in a World Where AI Writes It

A couple of months ago, during one of our AI Leadership Committee meetings, our superintendent asked a question that's been rattling around in my head ever since. It wasn't a gotcha — it was genuine curiosity, the kind of question a thoughtful leader asks when the world is shifting fast and they want to make sure the people in the room are keeping up. The question was something like: *If AI and large language models can now write code, and people can build software without actually writing any themselves, is it still worth teaching programming?*

I've seen versions of this question all over tech journalism lately. Senior engineers telling reporters they haven't written a line of code in months since tools like Claude Code became part of their daily workflow. The implication hangs in the air: if professionals aren't writing it, why are we making ninth-graders do it?

It's a fair question. I want to take it seriously rather than dismiss it — which means I have to start by pulling it apart a little.

---

The question rests on a premise that sounds reasonable but doesn't hold up: *If AI can do X, is teaching people to do X still valuable?* Apply that universally and you start getting into trouble fast. AI can write essays. Does that make English class obsolete? AI can generate images. Are art teachers next?

Nobody's really made that argument about writing yet — at least not loudly or seriously. And I think the reason is worth examining, because it reveals something important about what we actually believe education is *for*.

When an English teacher assigns an essay, they don't need the essay. Nobody's building an essay collection. The teacher needs the student to *write* the essay — because writing is thinking made visible. Until you externalize a thought in prose, it's easy to believe you understood something. Once you've written it out, the coherence (or lack of it) is sitting right there on the page for both of you to see. The product is almost beside the point. The process is the whole game.

We all know this. We just don't always say it out loud. Instead we sell writing as a practical skill — you'll need this for college applications, for cover letters — and that's fine, but it's secondary. The real argument for teaching writing has always been that it's how you learn to think carefully.

The same is true for art. The art project sitting on the table at the end of the semester matters less than what happened while the student was making it.

And the same is true for programming.

---

Here's where I think CS education is vulnerable in a way that English and art aren't, at least not yet. We have not done a great job of making the non-product argument for ourselves.

Writing has millennia of cultural backing. Art has centuries of theory and practice behind the idea that making things is inherently developmental. Computer science education — especially at the K-12 level — is newer, and we've leaned heavily on the job market argument: *Learn to code, get a good job.* That argument is both somewhat true and easily destabilized. If AI is going to write most of the code, and we've been telling students the point is to write code, we've handed the critics an easy win.

What we haven't said loudly enough is what algorithmic thinking actually develops: the capacity to decompose a problem into explicit, unambiguous steps; to discover that your understanding of something is incomplete the moment you try to instruct a computer to do it; to iterate, to debug, to hold a mental model of a system while you're working inside it. None of that goes away because an LLM can produce a working function. In fact — and here's the irony — it might matter more.

---

Because here's the thing about a world where AI agents write your code, generate your images, and draft your emails. What that world needs is people who are genuinely good at giving instructions. Not following them — *giving* them. Articulating requirements clearly enough that an AI system (or a human, or a team) can actually act on them. Understanding what you want precisely enough to evaluate whether what came back is close.

That is, nearly word for word, what we're teaching when we teach algorithmic thinking. The ability to look at a fuzzy problem and turn it into something explicit and actionable is exactly what programming practice builds. It's what happens when a student can't just tell their sprite to "move toward the other sprite" — they have to figure out the math and write it out, and the moment it doesn't work, they have to figure out *why*.

The superintendent's question might have looked like a challenge, but I think it's actually doing us a favor. It's asking us to stop selling CS education on the job market argument alone and start articulating what we've always believed is true: that learning to think algorithmically is worth doing for the same reasons learning to write is worth doing. Not because the product is valuable. Because the process changes how you think.

We haven't made that case well enough yet. Maybe this is a good time to start.
